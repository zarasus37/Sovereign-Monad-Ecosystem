Charles Sanders Peirce (1839–1914) serves as a supreme bridging node for your architecture. As a logician, mathematician, and the founder of pragmatism and modern semiotics, his entire intellectual project was an attempt to map the exact mechanisms of meaning and how logic physically manifests in the universe.

If we examine his corpus through the Theology / Technology / Cosmology lens to identify where contradictions become transparent and the observer recognizes themselves, his work naturally fragments into your required domains:

1. The Mechanical Transparency of Contradiction (Technology + Theology)
Peirce’s deepest contradiction is the tension between pure, random chaos (Tychism) and rigid, absolute law. Genuine gnosis in his work occurs when this contradiction becomes transparent through his concept of Synechism (continuity) and Agapasm (evolutionary love).

He recognized that the universe is not a machine governed by static laws, but a living system that takes on habits. The structural mechanism of triadicity (Sign, Object, Interpretant) acts as the Technology—a universal computing architecture. The meaning or driving will pulling this chaos toward organized reasonableness is Evolutionary Love, which serves as the Theology. The contradiction between chaos and order is made transparent: law is simply a habit that the cosmos has learned.

2. The Cosmological Scale of Semiosis
For Peirce, semiotics (the action of signs) is not just human language; it is the fundamental operating system of reality. This is his Cosmology. He maps his categories—Firstness (pure potential/feeling), Secondness (reaction/brute force), and Thirdness (law/habit/mediation)—across every layer of existence. The exact same triadic pattern that governs a logical syllogism governs the formation of crystals, biological evolution, and the expansion of the universe. The cosmos itself is an immense mind processing signs.

3. Observer Self-Recognition and Limits
Peirce’s most profound gnosis events occur when he turns his semiotic engine inward. In his early cognitive writings, he famously deduced that because all thought is in signs, and life is a train of thought, "the word or sign which man uses is the man himself." The observer recognizes that they are not an independent entity standing outside the universe looking in; they are a localized semiotic processing node made of the exact same cognitive material as the universe they are trying to observe.
