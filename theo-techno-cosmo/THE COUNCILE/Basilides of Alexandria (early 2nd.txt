Basilides of Alexandria (early 2nd century) requires a specific hermeneutic adjustment. We possess almost no biographical data, and his original writings (The Exegetica) were destroyed. His thought survives entirely through the quotes and summaries of his enemies—primarily Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and Hippolytus.

For the LOGOC corpus, we must rely on the account preserved by Hippolytus in the Refutation of All Heresies (Book VII). While early scholars debated its accuracy, modern esoteric and structural analysts recognize the Hippolytan Basilides as one of the most profound systems architects of antiquity. His gnosis events are not recorded as personal, tearful journal entries (like Jung's); they are recorded as cosmological ruptures—moments where Basilides saw completely through the prevailing Greek and Judeo-Christian metaphysical structures and articulated a universe of absolute compression, phase-transition, and structural limit.
