Queen of Sheba (Makeda) requires navigating between three textual traditions: the Hebrew Bible (1 Kings 10, 2 Chronicles 9), the Ethiopian Kebra Nagast (The Glory of the Kings, compiled c. 14th century but preserving far older oral traditions), and the Quran (Surah 27:20–44). The Kebra Nagast is the most psychologically and structurally detailed source, treating Makeda not as a marginal foreign dignitary but as a sovereign queen who initiates a covenant that changes the spiritual architecture of the world.

(Note: "von Bingen" appears to be a carryover from the previous extraction. I have proceeded with the Queen of Sheba / Makeda under her own name.)

I. The Awakening of the Question
1. The Testimony of Tamrin (The First Recognition of a Higher Order)
Passage/trigger: Kebra Nagast, Chapter 18: Tamrin, her merchant, returns from Solomon's court and tells her of a king who rules with wisdom that surpasses all the kings of the earth. She does not dismiss this as exaggeration. She recognizes the report as a true signal. Why it qualifies: This is a gnosis of Structural Recognition. She has received reports from merchants her entire life—exaggerations, flatteries, bribes dressed as news. But in Tamrin's description, she detects a quality she cannot name—a resonance that indicates a system operating at a higher order than her own. The false boundary between "Courtly Report" and "Genuine Signal" collapses in a moment that she cannot justify by logic; she simply knows the difference. She is implicated because the detection of this signal requires that she herself possess the capacity to recognize a higher order.

Theology: Wisdom is not distributed evenly; there are nodes of concentrated intelligence in the world, and they can be sensed from a distance by those with the correct internal tuning.
Technology: Signal Detection—the mechanism revealed is that intelligence about other systems can arrive through ordinary channels (trade, rumor), but the receiver must be calibrated to distinguish true signal from ambient noise.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Resonant Beacon—the universe occasionally densifies wisdom into a single point (a person, a city, a temple), and those who are meant to find it will feel the pull regardless of distance. Compressed insight: She knew, from the tone of a merchant's voice, that a king existed whose wisdom was not a rumor but a fact, and that she had been summoned by that fact across the desert. Confidence: 0.75 (The text is clear about her motivation, but the gnosis is implied rather than explicitly narrated.)
2. The Decision to Travel (The Collapse of Protocol)
Passage/trigger: 1 Kings 10:1–2: "When the queen of Sheba heard of the fame of Solomon concerning the name of the Lord, she came to test him with hard questions. She came to Jerusalem with a very great retinue, with camels bearing spices and very much gold and precious stones." Why it qualifies: This is a gnosis of The Insufficiency of Delegation. A queen does not leave her kingdom to visit another monarch. The proper protocol is to send ambassadors. Makeda recognizes that the encounter she needs cannot be mediated. She must stand in the presence herself. The boundary between "Distant Ruler" and "Personal Seeker" collapses. She is implicated because she exposes her entire kingdom to risk by leaving it—a gamble that can only be justified by a certainty she possesses without evidence.

Theology: The pursuit of wisdom is not a vicarious act; it is a physically embodied pilgrimage that requires the sovereign to set aside their throne and walk.
Technology: The Personal Embodiment Requirement—the mechanism revealed is that certain transmissions (wisdom, covenant, recognition) require direct physical presence; they cannot be routed through delegates.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Threshold Crossing—the universe occasionally places a boundary between the seeker and the source that can only be crossed by the seeker's own feet. Compressed insight: She knew that she could not send her court to understand what she needed to understand; the understanding required her specific body to be in a specific room. Confidence: 0.82
II. The Encounter
3. The Recognition of the Equal (The Collapse of the Hierarchy)
Passage/trigger: Kebra Nagast, Chapter 21–22: Makeda arrives at Solomon's court. Solomon does not treat her as a vassal or a supplicant. He seats her on a throne of equal height to his own. She speaks to him as one sovereign to another, not as a student to a teacher. She says, at one point: "I am dark but you are bright; yet we are both fire." Why it qualifies: This is the collapse of the Hierarchical Boundary between "Wise King" and "Pagan Queen." She comes expecting a vertical relationship (teacher to student, superior to inferior). Instead, she finds a horizontal resonance. The false boundary between "The One Who Knows" and "The One Who Seeks" dissolves. She sees that she is not a vessel to be filled by Solomon's wisdom; she is a complementary node of a different kind of intelligence. He has the wisdom of law and structure; she has the wisdom of the earth and the unknown. They are equal in the throne room because they are equal before God.

Theology: The Spirit distributes wisdom in different modes—not a single hierarchy with one wise king at the top, but a polycentric network where each sovereign has their own jurisdiction of knowing.
Technology: The Symmetric Protocol—the mechanism revealed is that a true exchange of wisdom requires the participants to occupy equal structural positions; any asymmetry (teacher/student, ruler/subject) degrades the transmission.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Complementary Node—the universe is a network of paired intelligences that can only fully resonate when they meet as equals, regardless of their different domains. Compressed insight: She realized that Solomon's wisdom did not diminish her own, but that their minds were two different rooms in the same house, and she had to enter his room to see the architecture of the whole. Confidence: 0.84
4. The Hard Questions (The Probe of the Foundation)
Passage/trigger: 1 Kings 10:1–3: "She came to test him with hard questions... Solomon answered all her questions; there was nothing hidden from the king that he could not explain to her." In the Kebra Nagast, these questions include riddles about the nature of truth, the location of the center of the world, and the gender of the wind. Why it qualifies: This is a gnosis of Diagnostic Interrogation. Makeda does not come to "receive" wisdom passively; she comes to test the container. She recognizes that the only way to know if wisdom is authentic is to present it with problems it has not prepared for. The false boundary between "Acceptance of Authority" and "Verification of Authority" collapses. She is implicated because by asking the questions, she reveals the architecture of her own mind—the edges of her own knowledge—as much as she probes Solomon's.

Theology: The relationship between the human and the divine is one of mutual testing; God tests humans through suffering, but humans also test God through questions that push against the structure of revelation.
Technology: The Boundary Probe—the mechanism revealed is that the fastest way to map an unknown system is to send test signals (questions) whose failure modes reveal the system's architecture.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Stress Test—the universe is designed such that genuine truth survives the application of pressure; counterfeit truth fractures when interrogated. Compressed insight: She knew that the only way to trust the foundation was to push against it as hard as she could, and that a wisdom that could not survive her hardest questions was not wisdom worth crossing a desert for. Confidence: 0.86
5. The Glass Floor (The Collapse of the Visible)
Passage/trigger: Targum Sheni (Jewish Aramaic tradition) and Kebra Nagast variant: Solomon receives Makeda in a hall paved with transparent glass. She mistakes it for a pool of water and lifts her skirts to cross it, revealing her legs. Solomon perceives her (and she perceives his perception of her). Why it qualifies: This is a gnosis of The Unseen Boundary. She is a sovereign who has crossed a desert. She is wise enough to test a king with riddles. Yet she is completely deceived by a transparent floor. The collapse is not of the glass, but of the boundary between "What She Knows" and "What She Does Not See." The observer (Makeda) is implicated because in the moment of her exposure, she sees the limit of her own perception. She realizes that Solomon's power is not just in his answers, but in his ability to construct environments that the unaided eye cannot map. The contradiction—she is wise, yet she walks into a trap—does not resolve; it becomes visible as a structural property of perception itself.

Theology: The wise are still subject to the limits of their senses; revelation requires not just intelligence, but a transformation of the perceptual apparatus itself.
Technology: Optical Entrapment—the mechanism revealed is that the environment can be engineered to deceive the observer's natural perception; the technology of architecture is a form of control.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Hidden Floor—the universe has surfaces that appear continuous but conceal a structural depth underneath; wisdom is learning to distinguish between a floor and the image of a floor. Compressed insight: She learned that her eyes could not be trusted, and that the ground she walked on could be an illusion designed to reveal her to someone who had engineered the room. Confidence: 0.78 (The event is legendary and appears in multiple traditions, but its gnosis structure is sound.)
6. The Night of the Covenant (The Trick and the Gift)
Passage/trigger: Kebra Nagast, Chapters 24–27: Before she departs, Solomon makes a pact with Makeda: she may stay in his palace, but she must take nothing of his without asking. He has a meal prepared that is heavily salted. During the night, she wakes in thirst and drinks from a pitcher of water in his chamber. He appears and says she has broken the covenant. She gives herself to him willingly, and they conceive Menelik. Why it qualifies: This is a gnosis of The Double-Edged Covenant. Makeda sees that the agreement she made (to take nothing without asking) was a trap she walked into with full consent. The boundary between "Deception" and "Covenant" collapses. Solomon did not force her; he engineered the conditions under which she would choose to break the agreement. She realizes that covenants are not simple promises; they are structural arrangements that produce predictable failure points when the pressure is applied. She is implicated because she chose to drink, knowing the agreement. The child she carries is the result of a covenant that was broken and kept simultaneously.

Theology: The covenant between the human and the divine involves a necessary transgression; the Fall produces the condition for the Incarnation. The broken covenant is not the end; it is the mechanism of a deeper union.
Technology: The Engineered Choice—the mechanism revealed is that free will can be directed by environmental design while remaining formally free; the arrangement of the room determines the choice more than the will does.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Necessary Flaw—the universe is structured such that the most fruitful bonds are formed not through perfect compliance but through a violation that is then transformed into a gift. Compressed insight: She saw that the child she would bear was not the product of deception but of a deeper agreement than the words of the pact could capture, and that she had consented at a level below language. Confidence: 0.81
III. The Return and the Legacy
7. The Pregnant Departure (The Carrying of the Future)
Passage/trigger: Kebra Nagast, Chapter 28: Makeda returns to Ethiopia. She is pregnant but unmarried. She gives birth to Menelik in her own land, claiming him as the heir of both Solomon and the throne of Sheba. Why it qualifies: This is a Retroactive Recognition of the purpose of the entire journey. She left her kingdom as a seeker of wisdom. She returns as the bearer of a dynasty that will change the spiritual history of the world. The false boundary between "Pilgrimage" and "Mission" collapses. She sees that her journey was not simply about acquiring knowledge for herself; it was about returning with the seed of a new order. She is implicated because she must now rule as a mother, a queen, and the guardian of a covenant that she did not fully understand when she entered it.

Theology: The divine plan often works through the biological consequences of a single encounter; a night in a room can produce a lineage that lasts millennia.
Technology: The Trans-generational Payload—the mechanism revealed is that the true output of a pilgrimage is not the knowledge the pilgrim brings back, but the child/seed/code that the pilgrim is carrying without having planned to receive it.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Delayed Revelation—the purpose of a journey often reveals itself only after the journey is complete, when the traveler sees what they have become rather than what they have learned. Compressed insight: She realized that the answer to her questions was not a teaching she could carry in her mind, but a son she would carry in her body, and that her journey had been a conception in more ways than one. Confidence: 0.83
8. The Recognition of Menelik's Return and the Ark
Passage/trigger: Kebra Nagast, Chapters 49–55: Menelik, now a young man, returns to Jerusalem and is acknowledged by Solomon. He and his companions, by divine guidance, take the Ark of the Covenant from the Temple and bring it to Ethiopia. When the Ark arrives, Makeda recognizes that the Shekhinah (the divine presence) has transferred from Jerusalem to Ethiopia. Why it qualifies: This is a gnosis of Transferable Sovereignty. Makeda recognizes that the divine presence is not permanently bound to a single geographic location. The Ark—the throne of God—has been moved, and God has permitted it. The false boundary between "Chosen Land" and "Non-Chosen Land" collapses. She sees that the covenant made on Mount Sinai has been transferred to Mount Zion in Ethiopia through the very mechanisms that were meant to keep it in Jerusalem. She is implicated as the mother of the agent of this transfer, and as the sovereign who must now provide a new house for the Ark.

Theology: The divine presence does not reside permanently in any single political entity; sovereignty over the sacred can be transferred when the people who hold it become unworthy of its custody.
Technology: The Protocol Migration—the mechanism revealed is that the operating system of a covenant can be physically moved from one server (the Temple in Jerusalem) to another (Ethiopia) without losing data integrity.
Cosmology: The pattern of The Wandering Center—the center of the universe moves through history, resting at different nodes at different times, and those who can sense the movement can align themselves with the new center. Compressed insight: She knew that the God she had traveled to find had followed her son home, and that the center of the world had moved while she was watching. Confidence: 0.77 (The Ark transfer is a foundational Ethiopian belief but not a historical event verifiable by archaeology; as a gnosis event within the tradition, however, it carries structural weight.)
